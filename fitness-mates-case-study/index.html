01 / THE BRIEF

What is Fitness Mates?

Fitness Mates is an Australia-based fitness marketplace designed to help people discover trainers, join events, and build local fitness communities through a unified digital ecosystem.

Founded by former professional rugby league players Luke Lewis and Jeremy Latimore, the vision for v1 combined marketplace booking with a social layer — connecting fitness through events, messaging, groups, and community-driven discovery. The project spanned mobile app, web platform, and marketing website design, creating a cohesive multi-surface experience for Australia's active fitness community.

"ClassPass meets Bumble BFF — for Australians who'd rather show up than scroll." — Founder brief, week one
02 / PRODUCT LEADERSHIP

Where the product decisions happened.

Four months, two engineers, one PM, a founding team with ClassPass-sized ambitions. The work happened in weekly rhythms — and the decisions in four specific rooms.

01

We cut the vision in half before design started.

Founders' v1 included events, bookings, messaging, groups, friend invites, and a feed. Two engineers and 16 weeks made it impossible. Kickoff was about subtraction.

KICKOFF · WK 0
02

We killed the highest-engagement idea.

Founders pushed for swipe-to-match — they'd seen Bumble's numbers. Three women in research called it predatory in a fitness context. A 5-user A/B settled it 5–0.

DESIGN REVIEW · WK 4
03

Density beat coverage.

Founders wanted three cities for press. With ~12 trainers in the pipeline, three cities meant empty Discover feeds. One city with workable supply density beat three with none.

DECISION GATE · WK 7
04

Timeline beat completeness.

Three weeks out, a founder asked: delay two weeks for a Stripe payouts dashboard? AUSactive nomination window was load-bearing. Dashboard shipped in v1.1 against real data.

DECISION GATE · WK 13

Documentary evidence — review screens from working sessions.

THE LESSON
The product wasn't built faster. The decisions were.
03 / THE CHALLENGE

A vibrant industry.
A fragmented experience.

The Australian fitness landscape has no shortage of energy — but its tools haven't caught up. Fitness seekers juggle apps to discover, book, message, and track. Trainers run businesses on Instagram DMs. The community moment never arrives.

3–4 apps

An average participant uses to find a class, book it, message the trainer, and remember to show up. We set out to collapse that to one.

$3.2B

The Australian fitness industry, 2023 — fragmented across gyms, studios, independents, and digital platforms.

0

Existing AU products combined social discovery, native booking, and trainer monetization in a single flow.

09 / THE SURFACES

Where the strategy meets the pixel.

Six surfaces did most of the work, organized around four sections: Events, Connect, Promotions, Profile. Each surface is annotated with the design decision behind it — not what it does, but why.

Web App · Dashboard

Same features, bigger canvas.

Where the mobile app shows one card per scroll, the web app shows eight. Where mobile gives you a single number, the web app charts the trend.

i
Three-column mate grid

The same Connect logic from mobile — laid out across a wider canvas without forcing scroll. Distance, mutuals, and shared interests sit in a glance.

ii
Earnings + bookings on one screen

Bulk actions and trend lines for trainers who manage their week at a desk, not a phone.

iii
One design system, two form factors

Cards, tokens, voice — identical to mobile. The web canvas just shows more of them at once.

Mobile + Web App · Discover

Discover

The participant's home. Distance-first, low-friction browse → book.

i
Physical / Virtual as the modality switch

Two pills sit above all other filters because modality is the most decisive disqualifier — no one wants to discover a Zoom yoga class when they're looking for an outdoor run.

ii
Distance + price chips on every card

Research showed distance and time were top-2 deciders. Price came fourth — present but secondary.

iii
Map view as a toggle, not the default

73% of users in usability testing opened list view first. Map became a deliberate move.

iv
Single floating create-listing CTA

Trainers and participants share Discover. The "+" surfaces only when signed in as a trainer.

Mobile + Web App · Listing detail

Listing detail

The booking moment. Trust above the fold, payment friction kept low.

i
Physical / Virtual modality + eligibility chips up top

"Physical" or "Virtual" badge surfaces immediately, then "Designed for", "Age required", "Gender allowed" — the four filters most likely to disqualify a user before they scroll.

ii
Available dates as a bookable grid

Trainer publishes 40+ recurring slots without scroll fatigue. Each cell carries spots-remaining as scarcity nudge.

iii
Trust-building tail: reviews + know the lister

Reviews and lister bio sit close to the booking CTA — exactly where hesitation peaks for stranger-trainer bookings.

iv
Sticky bottom CTA · price + book in one bar

Resolves a usability finding where 3/5 testers missed an inline CTA mid-page. Price stays anchored next to the action.

Mobile + Web App · Trainer profile & earnings

Trainer profile & earnings

The supply-side home. Built around the one number a trainer wants to see first.

i
Total earnings up top, not buried in a tab

The number a trainer opens the app to check. Drives session-add behavior more than any nudge we tested.

ii
Listings, bookings, team — all one tap deep

The five highest-frequency trainer tasks sit on the first screen. No nested settings page for what they do every week.

iii
Stripe + bank linking grouped, not scattered

"Wallet management" and "Bank account & Stripe" sit adjacent. Money in, money out, in one mental model.

Mobile + Web App · Event creation & listing flow

Creating listings

The listing flow was designed to help trainers and businesses publish events quickly while keeping information structured and easy to trust.

i
Flexible creator types

Users can create listings as a trainer, business, or individual — supporting different kinds of fitness experiences within one system.

ii
Visual-first event setup

Listing images appear early in the flow to quickly communicate the event's energy and training style.

iii
Structured event details

Event type, venue, interests, scheduling, and participation preferences are grouped into clear, manageable steps.

iv
Built for recurring fitness routines

Recurring event support helps trainers manage ongoing classes instead of repeatedly creating one-time sessions.

Mobile + Web App · Social discovery & community

Connect & community

The social layer designed to make fitness feel less individual — and more accountable, local, and consistent.

i
Interest-first discovery instead of random matching

Users discover fitness mates through shared goals, workout styles, and nearby activity — creating more relevant connections than generic social feeds.

ii
Trainer ↔ client communication built into the product

Messaging removes the gap between discovery and coordination. Scheduling, follow-ups, motivation, and session planning happen in one continuous flow.

iii
Community designed around participation, not scrolling

The experience prioritises showing up over passive engagement. Nearby mates, mutual interests, and lightweight interaction patterns encourage real-world fitness participation.

Website · Get fit, get connected.

Get fit, get connected.

A marketing-led web platform. Designed for sign-up conversion, not feature parity with the app.

i
One headline, two paths

"Earn money as a lister" or "Browse & book activities." The web hero collapses every visitor into one of two intents.

ii
Filters mirror the app, but lighter

Sessions / Package / Team chips repeat from mobile, so a web→app handoff feels native. Discovery on web is a teaser.

iii
Listings grid for SEO, not browsing

Crawlable listing cards drive organic acquisition. Web's job in v1 is to feed the app, not replace it.

10 / VALIDATION

How I knew it was working.

Five unmoderated tests, three moderated trainer sessions in their own studios, one cohort of pre-launch beta users. Three findings changed the product before launch.

Test setup + pre-launch beta

Eight usability sessions in week 9, plus a 2-week pre-launch beta with the 12 Sydney trainers we'd onboarded as cold-start supply. Two trainers churned in the first week — both said the listing-creation flow was too long. That's what triggered the 14-field → 7-field cut. Both came back after the simplification.

14 / WHAT I'D DO DIFFERENTLY

Two regrets and a bet.

Every project ships with regret. Two belong to v1. The third question is what v2 has to answer.

1.

Push back harder on listing-flow length.

We optimized for completeness. We should have optimized for time-to-first-listing. Even at seven fields, 12% of trainers abandoned. v2 should be three fields and a "complete later" prompt.

2.

Start the design system on day one.

I built components reactively, screen by screen. By month three I had to refactor 40+ components. Two days of token + primitive setup at the start would have saved two weeks of cleanup at the end.

THE BET
v1 optimised for trainer activation — the harder side of any marketplace, and the data confirms it worked. v2 has to be consumer-side. If retention doesn't compound by month 6, the trainers we worked so hard to acquire start to churn. Empty classes are worse than no classes.
Fitness products don't fail because users lack motivation. They fail because consistency is harder to design than engagement.
